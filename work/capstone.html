Usability enhancement of
e-governance portals

A lightweight browser extension that allows developers to directly address and rectify usability issues with portals, contributing toward a more accessible, transparent, and efficient e-governance ecosystem in India.

OVERVIEW

Usability challenges present in existing Government of India (GoI) portals pose significant obstacles to citizen engagement and empowerment. The portals have become notoriously known for being unusable. Most GoI websites cover fundamental services such as transport booking and national identity/license application. A subset of these sites even trickle down to state-level services that deal with grievance redressal or data access, for example.

Hence, the aforementioned lack of usability in such a wide range of websites is detrimental to the timely handling of citizen-centric needs, many of which are time-sensitive. While issues are regularly reported, they are often not catered to for multiple sociotechnical reasons.

RESEARCH

User survey

To understand the frequency and nature of issues on e-governance websites, I designed a survey for users who largely fit their target consumer profile: middle to old-aged individuals. The survey asked respondents to list the sites they frequently visited and describe their usual experience with the same – specific pain points and/or favourable features. Then, it asked them to list functionality requests that could be implemented to resolve the aforementioned pain points or to improve user experience as a whole. Lastly, it gauged what proportion of the respondents would be able and willing to install the browser extension tool. Case study

The survey illuminated how IRCTC might be an ideal choice for a case study as many feature requests and complaints were specific to the site. For context: IRCTC is a train booking service owned by GoI and used by millions. Thus, I tried to experientially gauge the critical issues associated with a foundational user flow on the site — purchasing a ticket.

My experience on the site confirmed the survey’s findings. The IRCTC website displayed a wide range of issues related to content redundancy, inconsistent styling, and opaque information.

IDEATION

By using IRCTC as a specific case, I was able to narrow down the specific design and optimisation interventions required according to usability design standards, as shown in the table. These interventions were picked based on the expectation that they can eventually be applied across other GoI e-governance sites without significant modification. IMPLEMENTATION

The designed tool works in the context of a popup window that operates on top of the target tab. All user input and intervention preferences are routed through this popup, which then communicates with the background service worker script present in the extension. The background script carries out all Chrome API calls such as executing a script, function, or creating a new tab. The content script relates the local extension instance to the real webpage instance by modifying the existing elements and DOM structure.

This framework allows for real-time, programmatic script injection that is entirely controlled by the user’s actions on the popup and the main frame. An interface can produce a button, tooltip, or change styles and see the immediate effect of their action on the page with 1 click. The popup window itself is designed to minimise data entry while also giving the developer a wide scope of intervention options. Some of the existing interventions required more parameterization and fine-tuning than the baseline DOM API functions offered, such as removing an element through its display or visibility styling. NEXT STEPS

If given a chance to revise or build upon my work, I would focus on the following:

  • Including developer input: While my research extensively logged the requirements of standard portal users, it left out a major stakeholder – front-end developers. By including their insights, this tool can be made better suited to a development environment.

  • More focus on optimisation: Currently, the tool prioritises usability over optimisation. However, by implementing optimisation features, we can resolve more technical user pain points such as loading time and the lack of local storage, which eventually contribute positively to usability.

  • Extend the use-case: In addition to a live prototyping tool, this can act as a robust feature suggestion pipeline, especially for the GoI. For example, automatically converting modifications into a comprehensive proposal report.

TAKEAWAYS

This project allowed me to understand and implement a traditional HCI research cycle. I started with defining a problem statement and then supplementing it with user research. Here, I understood the importance of context and regional research, as India’s political and economic background played a significant role in my design choices. During development, I went through multiple rounds of ideation and sourced an expansive list of enhancements but it was crucial to cover the bases first. Instead of attempting to introduce fancy features, I tried to first ensure that fundamental usability requirements, such as removing and restyling elements, were satisfied.
