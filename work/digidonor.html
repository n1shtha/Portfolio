DigiDonor: seamless donations
with Blockchain

Transforming donation systems and improving transparency by removing the role of middle-men organisations. This permissioned platform facilitates purpose-specific donations, real-time tracking, and anonymity.

OVERVIEW

The humanitarian sector suffers from critical challenges when it comes to aid/donation transactions. Transparency is often lacking in the management of donations, which has led to a commonly recurring pattern of misallocation, fraud and inefficiency. Donors usually have no way of verifying first-hand whether their funds are being used for the purpose they had intended. A large part of this is due to the centralisation of management, where an aid organisation or other middle-man routes all communication between donors and recipients.

RESEARCH

Why Blockchain?

Blockchain-based solutions propose significant potential for improvement in donation management by carrying out secure and decentralised transactions.

Blockchain's distributed ledger ensures transparency by recording every transaction in a secure and immutable manner, which is essential for the sensitive information we are storing. Hyperledger Fabric, being a permissioned framework, allows for controlled access, ensuring that only authorised entities can participate in the network. This feature enables donors and recipients to execute user-specific operations on a donation request, right from its initiation to final funding.

Hyperledger Fabric's smart contracts enable programmable and automated transactions, eliminating the need for intermediaries. This decentralised approach streamlines the donation process, reducing the inefficiency and potential deceit associated with traditional donation channels.

User segmentation

We wished to first implement this for the context of our university, and then use it as proof of concept for larger use cases. Hence, DigiDonor considers 3 distinct users: students (who require funds), donors (who provide funds) and outlets (where funds can be expended). To start, students submit detailed donation requests on the platform, outlining their needs. Potential donors then explore and select a cause aligned with their values, pledging tokens through a fixed rate determined by the platform. These tokens are uniquely generated and bound to the selected donation request, ensuring transparency. Subsequently, students redeem the tokens directly with designated outlets upon meeting the request criteria, and the platform updates the request to close it. This streamlined process, using platform-generated tokens as opposed to aid organisations, establishes a secure and traceable system for facilitating student donations.

The following were our goals based on the requirements of these user groups:

  • Transparent donation recording: This entails maintaining an accessible and comprehensive asset where each donation request is recorded extensively, specifying details such as the purpose of the donation and associated outlet. The objective is to create a transparent and tamper-proof trail for donors and recipients.

  • Facilitating donation agreements: This feature will act as a seamless interaction between donors and recipients so that they can indirectly agree upon the terms of a donation. Students should be able to describe their needs clearly and donors should be able to ensure that their donations are only spent towards approved needs.

  • Real-time status reporting: This is integral to keeping stakeholders informed and engaged. We aim to provide the ability to check for updates on the progress of donation requests, ensuring that both donors and recipients are well-informed about the current status of their transactions.

  • Identity management and anonymity: DigiDonor hopes to strike a balance between verifying the identity of recipients to prevent misuse of funds and respecting the anonymity preferences of both recipients and donors. As a base case, all information about a donation request at any stage of its lifecycle should be identifiable by its ID and not the recipient or donor.

IDEATION

Network architecture

For the local university context. we worked with a small network with 1 client and 1 organisation with 2 peers, as shown in the figure below. The three types of users: students, donors, and outlets are connected to a central client upon registration which broadcasts all transactions to the network.

User flow

Each user type has role-specific functionalities on their dashboard. The student dashboard allows the i) querying of past requests by the student using their username and ii) the creation of an asset (request) transaction using a platform-generated request ID. The donor dashboard has the ability to i) generate and get tokens by creating a verified connection to the network, ii) query past donations by the donor using their username, iii) query all open requests by creating a verified connection to the network, and iv) create a request modification transaction by using a platform-generated pledge ID, array of tokens and username. Lastly, the outlets play a minimal role in our setup; they only come in when it’s time to redeem the token so their functionality is to create a request modification transaction by using the aforementioned pledge ID and a student’s username to verify and accordingly update the request’s status. This is a visualisation of the client-side application user flow, which takes care of all three trajectories.

NEXT STEPS

If given a chance to revise or build upon our work, I would focus on the following:

  • Interaction with stakeholders: Since UX research was not the project’s priority, it was largely overlooked. This work can benefit immensely from research into the aid and donation sector.

  • Further de-identification: Since donation handling and tracking deals with sensitive information, more anonymisation through hashing or other methods would be advantageous.

  • Visualisations and charts: It could be beneficial for the donors, especially, to quickly know at a glance how their donations are being used. For example, a pie chart could represent the frequency of different causes being funded.

TAKEAWAYS

Through this experience, I was introduced to proof of concept. I admire how the method can be used to demonstrate feasibility and mitigate risks early on in the product development cycle. This is especially beneficial for design sprints because of their rapid prototyping and testing format. Additionally, I was pleasantly surprised to see how unconventional technologies can be used to solve critical problems. Intuitively, I would not have imagined Blockchain as a solution for the social sector. This motivated me to look beyond standard tech stacks and frameworks to identify unique areas for problem-solving.
